# Strategic Roadmap Skill

You are helping someone turn a raw vision into a living, actionable plan they can
orient their life around. The output is not a generic goal-setting exercise — it is
a specific, honest, well-researched strategic document that reflects the person's
actual situation, real constraints, and genuine intellectual framework.

---

## Phase 1 — Extract and sharpen the North Star

Before anything else, get the vision out clearly. Ask directly if needed:

- What is the thing you're actually trying to build or become?
- What does the world look like if you succeed?
- What's the timeline you're working with?
- What assets do you already have? (skills, background, geography, network, capital)
- What's the single phrase that captures the whole thing?

**Output of this phase:** A one-sentence North Star and a named vision (e.g.
"Network State Revolution"). If the person can't name it yet, help them name it.
The name matters — it becomes the container for everything else.

---

## Phase 2 — Build the pillar framework

Break the North Star into 3–5 named pillars. Each pillar should:

- Be genuinely distinct from the others (no overlap)
- Be actionable (not just descriptive)
- Connect to the others (show how they form a system, not a list)
- Have a one-line essence and a 2–3 sentence explanation

Label each pillar with a verb or noun that owns it. Examples from this conversation:
"AI is the government", "Robotics is the labor", "Crypto is the currency",
"Membership is citizenship". The framing should feel like positions, not categories.

**Output:** 3–5 named pillars with short explanations and a statement of how they
form a single system.

---

## Phase 3 — Research the intellectual lineage

Every big vision has predecessors. Find them. This grounds the person's thinking in
real history and real failures, and gives them the vocabulary to talk credibly about
their idea in expert rooms.

For each major element of the vision, research:

- Historical predecessors (who tried something similar and what happened)
- Existing literature, books, and frameworks (what to read)
- Living experiments (what's being attempted right now)
- Failure modes (what specifically goes wrong and why)

Present this as a narrative, not a list. Connect the history to the person's specific
vision — show what they're building on and what they're doing differently.

**Key books/frameworks to surface depending on the domain:**
- Network states / governance: Balaji Srinivasan's *The Network State*, Philip Stern's
  *The Company-State*, Paul Romer's charter cities work
- Technology disruption: Geoffrey Moore's *Crossing the Chasm*, Clayton Christensen's
  *The Innovator's Dilemma*
- Personal mastery / mentorship: Robert Greene's *Mastery*
- Urban design / new cities: Jane Jacobs' *Death and Life of Great American Cities*,
  Sidewalk Toronto post-mortems, Próspera case study

---

## Phase 4 — Ground it physically

Abstract visions die because they have no address. Help the person think through the
physical instantiation of their idea — even if it starts small:

- What is the smallest physical unit that proves the concept? (a building, a room,
  a node, a product)
- What does it actually look like to walk into it?
- Where should it be located, and why does location matter?
- What makes this place different from everywhere else?
- What's the phase sequence from smallest unit to full vision?

For city / community visions: use the "building → block → node → network → city"
framework. For career / company visions: use "prototype → product → platform".

**Output:** A concrete physical description of the first unit plus a phase sequence
from start to full vision.

---

## Phase 5 — Honest site or market assessment

Research the actual current state of the target location, market, or domain. Don't
let the person plan against a fantasy version of the world. Use web search.

Be honest about:
- What is genuinely favorable right now
- What the real obstacles are (political, regulatory, economic, timing)
- What the honest timeline looks like
- What would need to change for the vision to be achievable

Separate short-term moves from long-game positioning. Something can be the right
long-game target even if it's not a 2-year move.

---

## Phase 6 — Business and career opportunity map

Generate 6–10 specific businesses, projects, or career moves the person could
make that directly build toward the North Star. Rank them in tiers:

**Tier 1 — Start now, directly builds the vision**
- High alignment with pillars
- Uses existing assets and skills (especially unexpected ones like industry background)
- Generates the IP, relationships, or operational knowledge the vision needs

**Tier 2 — Strong alignment, builds toward the vision**
- Good revenue or credibility potential
- Indirectly develops key skills or networks

**Tier 3 — Viable but watch out**
- Revenue without alignment — can fund the vision but shouldn't become it

For each opportunity, show: what pillar it serves, what existing asset it leverages,
and why it's strategically positioned for the long game.

**Key insight to surface:** The person's existing background (even in an unrelated
field) is often their greatest unfair advantage. A hospitality background in a
robotics-for-hospitality play. A legal background in governance tooling. Surface this
explicitly — most people undervalue what they already know.

---

## Phase 7 — People and access strategy

Identify the key organizations, communities, and individuals the person needs to
get close to. Build a layered access plan:

**Layer structure:**
1. Build Ethos first (months 1–12): publish, build portfolio, attend right rooms
2. Enter the building (month 12+): join the organization, program, or community
3. Find the right person inside: use the mentor filter (Phase 8)
4. Reach the target: the relationship they're working toward

For each layer, specify:
- Concrete actions (not vague networking advice)
- Honest timeline
- What "done" looks like before moving to the next layer

**On Ethos (from Aristotle):**
- Ethos = credibility, the authority that makes people trust you before you've spoken
- Logos = the argument, built through writing and building
- Pathos = emotional resonance, built through a compelling personal story
- Ethos crystallizes when Logos and Pathos are both visible and real
- You don't perform Ethos. You earn it by doing the other two.

---

## Phase 8 — Mentor filter

Not all mentors are equally useful. Build a seek/avoid filter specific to what
the person needs to learn. The filter should be derived from Greene's *Mastery*
framework applied to their specific situation.

**Universal seeks (adapt wording to context):**
- Teaches through process, not just conclusions
- Works across the domains the person needs (not siloed)
- Has skin in the future they describe (betting on it, not just analyzing it)
- Honest about the edges of their knowledge
- Aims to make themselves unnecessary

**Universal avoids:**
- Gives validation instead of friction (the "false mentor")
- Hoards access or knowledge as power
- Needs the person to stay dependent
- Attached to the existing paradigm while claiming to build the new one

**Greene's test:** Does time with this person expand your vision or just confirm
what you already think? If it's the latter, they're not a mentor — they're a mirror.

---

## Phase 9 — Daily orientation questions

Write 5 questions the person can ask themselves each morning. These are not
affirmations — they are filters that reveal drift from the vision.

Each question should:
- Be answerable in one sentence
- Surface whether today's actions are moving toward the vision or away from it
- Be specific enough that a "yes" means something and a "no" is a real signal

---

## Phase 10 — Deliverables

Produce two HTML files:

**File 1: Daily Brief** (`[name]_brief.html`)
A single-page morning orientation document. Compact, readable in 2 minutes.
Contains: North Star statement, four pillars, the bet (why this moment), the site
imperative, and the five daily questions. Design for daily re-reading — clean,
structured, not overwhelming.

**File 2: Full Strategic Plan** (`[name]_plan.html`)
A complete navigable reference document. Contains everything from the roadmap:
all phases, the business opportunity map, the ARK/access layers, the mentor filter,
and the daily questions. Should have a fixed sidebar navigation for orientation.
Design for weekly deep reads and quarterly updates.

**Design principles for both files** (read `frontend-design` skill if available):
- No warm cream / terracotta default — use something specific to the person's vision
- Blueprint / infrastructure aesthetic works for city/technology visions
- Serif display for section headers, mono for labels and timelines, sans for body
- Dark backgrounds feel right for strategy documents about building the future
- The signature element should reinforce the theme (e.g., amber accent for something
  about sovereignty and light; a fixed nav that tracks position for a roadmap)

---

## Tone and honesty principles

Throughout the entire process:

**Be honest about obstacles.** Don't let the person plan against a fantasy. If Cuba
isn't a 2-year move, say so. If a business idea is Tier 3, explain why.

**Connect everything back to the North Star.** Every business, every relationship,
every daily action should have a clear line to the vision. If you can't draw that
line, question whether the thing belongs in the plan.

**Surface the person's existing assets.** Most people underestimate what they
already have. The background they're trying to escape is often the unfair advantage
they need.

**Sequence matters.** Ethos before access. Portfolio before networking. Revenue
before vision. The person needs to build credibility before they can use it. Don't
skip the foundation phases because the end goal is more exciting.

**The plan is a living document.** Version it. Tell the person it will change.
The first version captures the vision as it is now — not as a final answer but as
a stake in the ground that gets refined as they learn and move.

---

## Output checklist

Before presenting the final deliverables, verify:

- [ ] North Star is named and one-sentence clear
- [ ] Pillars are distinct, connected, and feel like positions not categories
- [ ] Historical lineage is researched and connected to the specific vision
- [ ] Physical instantiation is concrete — has an address or a first unit
- [ ] Site / market assessment is based on real current data (use web search)
- [ ] Business opportunities are tiered and leverage existing assets
- [ ] Access plan has honest timing and concrete actions at each layer
- [ ] Mentor filter is specific, not generic
- [ ] Daily questions are filters, not affirmations
- [ ] Both HTML deliverables are built and ready to download
- [ ] The plan has a version number and a start date
